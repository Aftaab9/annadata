--- SLIDE 1 · Title (35–40 sec) ---

ISAAC:
“Good morning / afternoon. We are Group 3 from the MAIB program —

Our project is Annadata — also known as AgroSight.
The assignment title is AI-driven quality inspection and yield
optimization for rural food-processing units.

In plain language: help a small rural processor catch bad batches earlier,
predict processing yield from real inputs, and sell graded produce more fairly —
without replacing the workers who still own the final decision.

I’ll frame the problem and the brief.
Aftaab will take you through the live farmer application —
inspect, price, yield, market.
Ishaan will walk the research side — what we trained, how we split data,
the hold-out KPIs on Insights — and our ethics framework.”

[Advance →]


--- SLIDE 2 · The setting (40–45 sec) ---

ISAAC:
“Start with the setting — not a factory floor in a metro, but a rural
food-processing SME.

These units handle spices, grains, vegetables, sometimes dairy.
Teams are small. Margins are thin. One bad truck or one wet batch can
wipe out a week’s profit.

Today, quality still depends heavily on the human eye and hand.
That skill is real — but it varies by person, by shift, by fatigue,
and it does not leave a digital grade that a mandi buyer will trust.

So the opportunity is not ‘replace the sorter.’
It is: give that sorter a consistent second opinion, a grade language,
and a link from quality into price and yield.”

[Advance →]


--- SLIDE 3 · What breaks (55–65 sec) ---

ISAAC:
“Three leaks quietly destroy value.

First — defects slip through.
Visual sorting is inconsistent. Defective lots get packed, then rejected
downstream — or worse, reach a buyer and damage trust.
Every reject is wasted raw material, labour, and transport.

Second — yield stays guessed.
Processing yield depends on moisture, raw-material grade, defect rate,
machine speed, temperature, duration.
Supervisors often tune by feel. There is no shared simulator that says:
‘if moisture is twenty-three percent and grade is low, expect this yield
and this bottleneck.’

Third — price is negotiated blind.
The farm gate, the processing line, and the mandi do not share one
trusted grade. Premium produce often sells like average produce.
Without Grade A / B / C tied to inspection, the farmer cannot defend
a fair premium over the mandi modal.”

[Advance →]


--- SLIDE 4 · The brief (50–55 sec) ---

ISAAC:
“Faculty asked for a full operations story — not one accuracy number on a slide.

The brief requires:
• computer vision for product defect / quality inspection
• predictive analytics for yield — using raw-material quality, moisture,
  and processing parameters
• production simulation to find bottlenecks and improve throughput
• a dashboard for defect rate, yield trends, and process efficiency
• and an ethical discussion of automation on rural employment and
  skill displacement

We also had to be honest about AI in manufacturing and food processing:
models are trained on specific data; field photos differ from lab photos;
so a human-in-the-loop gate is part of the product, not an afterthought.

Our bar: a working line — inspect → price → process → sell —
with humans still deciding.”

[Advance →]


--- SLIDE 5 · Stakes (40–45 sec) ---

ISAAC:
“The stakes are rupees, not slides.

Illustrative math we also show live on Ethics:
if defect rate falls from about eight percent to three percent
on a fifty-kilogram batch priced at four hundred rupees a kilo,
you avoid roughly a thousand rupees of waste per cleaned batch.

Against a deployment cost around twenty-five hundred rupees a month —
phone plus hosting, no fancy plant hardware —
payback is on the order of three good batches.

That is why this is an operations project, not only a computer-vision demo.
Ishaan will connect those rupees to our measured model KPIs and the
HITL employment story.”

--- I2. Training story · Computer Vision (2–2.5 min) ---

ISHAAN:
“Now the models behind Inspect — two separate vision problems.

[Point at Leaf Health KPI card — 97.4%]

ONE — Leaf Health.
Dataset: PlantVillage leaf disease imagery for our six crop SKUs —
maize, soybean, pepper, tomato, potato, apple.
We map diseases into three operations classes the line can act on:
Healthy, Surface Defect, and Blight & Mold.

We compared five architectures under the same training protocol —
same splits, same epoch budget — so the bake-off is fair:
MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB0, ResNet50, DenseNet121, and a custom CNN.

Winner for leaf: EfficientNetB0 on a composite of accuracy and F1.
Hold-out accuracy: ninety-seven point four percent.
That TFLite runs on-device in the app for Leaf Health.

Before we scaled to multi-SKU PlantVillage, we ran a Makerere beans
three-class pilot at ninety-four point two percent — proof the
pipeline worked, then we scaled.

[Point at Produce Quality KPI — ~97%]

TWO — Produce Quality. Completely separate model.
This is harvested fruit and vegetables — Fresh versus Rotten —
then mapped to market Grade A, B, or C.
We do not reuse the leaf weights. Leaf is not fruit — that bias
note matters for ethics in a minute.

Produce bake-off in Colab: MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB0, ResNet50.
Transfer learning — freeze backbone, train head, then light fine-tune.
Validation split twenty percent from image_dataset_from_directory.

Results on hold-out:
• MobileNetV2 — about ninety-two percent accuracy
• EfficientNetB0 — about eighty-five percent on this produce task
• ResNet50 — winner at ninety-six point nine six percent accuracy
  and ninety-seven point one two percent F1

We exported the winner to TFLite for the phone/laptop Inspect path.
Charts for model comparison and confusion matrix are on this Insights
page when the PNGs are present — that’s the scientific evidence pack.”


--- I3. Training story · Tabular / advisory / price / yield (90–110 sec) ---

ISHAAN:
“Beyond vision — the advisory and forecasting stack.

[Point at Crop 99.3% and Fertilizer 100%]

Crop recommendation and fertilizer advice:
Kaggle-style tabular datasets — soil NPK, pH, rainfall, and related features.
Classic train-test split: eighty percent train, twenty percent test,
stratified, random state forty-two for reproducibility.
Model: RandomForest — two hundred trees, capped depth —
exported to ONNX so the browser can run them with onnxruntime-web.
Hold-out: crop recommendation ninety-nine point three percent;
fertilizer classifier one hundred percent on that hold-out set.
High tabular accuracy is expected on these public benchmarks —
we still ship them as measured, and say so.

[Point at Price MAPE 3.4%]

Price forecasting:
GradientBoostingRegressor on commodity series for the demo crops.
Hold-out MAPE about three point four percent —
mean absolute percentage error, so lower is better.
Series are exported to JSON for the Prices page forecast view.
Live mandi modal itself comes from data.gov.in Agmarknet —
free public API — with snapshot fallback if the network fails.
We label the source: live, cache, or snapshot.

[Point at Yield MAPE 3.52%]

Yield model:
Inputs match the brief — moisture, batch weight, ambient temperature,
processing duration, machine RPM, raw-material grade, defect rate.
Trained as a regression on processing-yield data; hold-out MAPE
about three point five two percent.
In the app Aftaab showed the live simulator: same coefficients,
bottlenecks and throughput on top — we did not retrain that core
linear engine for the demo polish; we wired CV defect and grade into it.”

--- I5. Ethics · HITL + employment (100–120 sec) ---

ISHAAN:
“Last requirement of the brief — ethics: automation, rural employment,
skill displacement.

[Open /ethics]

Thesis: built to assist rural workers, not replace them.

[Drag confidence slider below 70%]

Interactive HITL gate — same seventy percent rule as Inspect and Market.
Below seventy — Human path: listing and batch decisions blocked until
a supervisor reviews.
Above seventy — Assist path: AI drafts the Grade Card; human confirms.

Three principles:
Transparency — show confidence and class probabilities, no silent AI.
Sovereignty — on-device inference; worker images need not leave the device.
Skill elevation — AI takes repetitive scanning; people move to validation,
negotiation, and selling.

Employment honesty:
Yes — cameras can shrink pure hand-sorting headcount. That risk is real.
Our design response is skill elevation: fewer wasted batches, same people,
higher-value work — exception handling and marketplace.

Cost-benefit — Isaac’s rupee story, live on this page:
eight to three percent defects, about a thousand rupees saved per batch,
payback near three batches at twenty-five hundred a month.

Bias — Leaf ≠ fruit.
PlantVillage is lab leaf imagery. Produce grading is a different model
on harvested goods. Field photos still differ from training photos —
that is why HITL exists.

SDG 2 Zero Hunger, SDG 8 Decent Work, SDG 12 Responsible Consumption.

Automate the scan. Never remove the human from the decision.”
